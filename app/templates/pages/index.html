What are the main types of skin cancer?

The main types of skin cancer are basal cell carcinoma (BCC), squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), and melanoma. BCC and SCC are more common and less aggressive, while melanoma is rarer but more dangerous because it can spread to other parts of the body.

What are the risk factors for skin cancer?

Risk factors for skin cancer include excessive sun exposure, a history of sunburns, fair skin, a family history of skin cancer, a large number of moles, and using tanning beds. People with weakened immune systems are also at higher risk.

How can I tell if a mole or spot on my skin is cancerous?

Check moles and spots using the ABCDE rule: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter larger than 6mm, and Evolving changes in the mole. Any changes or new symptoms like itching or bleeding should be examined by a dermatologist.

How is skin cancer diagnosed?

Skin cancer is diagnosed by a dermatologist who examines the skin. If a suspicious spot is found, a biopsy is performed, where a small sample of skin is taken and analyzed in a lab to check for cancer cells.

What are the chances of surviving skin cancer?

The survival rate for skin cancer varies by type and stage. Early-detected basal cell and squamous cell carcinomas have high cure rates. Melanoma survival rates depend on early detection and prompt treatment, emphasizing the importance of regular skin checks.

How often should I have my skin checked by a dermatologist?

It’s recommended to have a full-body skin exam by a dermatologist annually, or more often if you have a higher risk of skin cancer. Regular self-exams are also important to monitor any changes in your skin.
